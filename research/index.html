Research

My research interests include formal methods and their application on software verification, as well as the development of logical foundations for programming languages. I have always been interested in pure mathematical reasoning and I found in these fields of Computer Science a way to nurture that interest while contributing with a direct impact in society. Threading on the boundary between theory and practice is something I wish to explore at this early stage of my career.

Currently, I am working on incorrectness logic for scalable bug detection in libraries that rely on internal use of unsafe features. In particular, my focus is on the Rust programming language, building on the type soundness result of RustBelt. The main goal of this project is to develop a sound and scalable technique for automatic detection of type unsoundness in internally unsafe Rust libraries.

I have experience with the Rocq proof assistant and with the Iris framework. My MSc thesis consisted in the verification of a concurrent skip list algorithm in Iris. The Rocq development is publicly available and also contains an alternative definition of atomic triples within the Iris logic.

and I created CoqPyt, a Python tool for interacting with Rocq (previously known as Coq) via extraction of contextual information from proofs, as well as insertion and removal of steps in proofs. CoqPyt allowed the construction of a dataset used to train and evaluate Rango, a state-of-the-art LLM-based proof synthesizer.
